DOING THE RIGHT THING

Compliance means that, as an organization and as individual employees, we follow the rules that govern the Hospital and how it does business. It means following federal, state, and local laws, as well as Hospital policies and procedures.

These rules are for things such as, Hospital billing for the services it provides to patients; interacting with patients and their families; conducting relationships with our suppliers; and following the legal requirements of the agencies that license the Hospital.

In a nutshell, compliance means doing the right thing. Everyone at CHOP should be familiar with our Organizational Ethics Statement and our Compliance Standards of Conduct, both of which can be found on the Compliance intranet site.

Compliance Program & Your Role

CHOP'S Compliance Program

CHOP'S Compliance Program consists of:
  • CHOP’S Office of Compliance and Privacy
  • Written standards guiding our work and relationships
  • Reporting options for compliance concerns / violations
  • Compliance Monitoring or “regular checkups”
  • Regular compliance training
  • Compliance problem or violation investigation
  • Advising / disciplining on compliance violations
  • And...YOU!

Janet S. Holocombe
CHOP’s Chief Compliance Officer “Each day, no matter what your role, you can help ensure all CHOP activities follow the legal and ethical standards that apply to our organization and protect our patients.”

Your Role in Compliance

You must follow the policies, rules and standards in your department and Hospital-wide that are put in place to protect our patients and the institution. It is your obligation to do the right thing by speaking up and raising any compliance questions or concerns.

You must report any conduct you reasonably believe is or may be illegal, unethical, in violation of CHOP policy, or otherwise questionable. CHOP policy forbids retaliation for raising compliance concerns in good faith. You can learn more about whistleblower protection on the Compliance intranet site.

You must participate in mandatory training programs to learn about compliance at CHOP.

Work Area Rules & Scenarios

Work Areas Rules

Regardless of your work area (i.e. patient care, research operations and practices, patient billing, etc.), there is potential for the occurrence of legal, ethical, professional standard, or CHOP policy and procedure violations.

Familiarize yourself with the rules in your area, and ask questions if you have concerns.

A Note About Vendors:

The Hospital has policies that govern our interaction with companies that do business or seek to do business with CHOP. These policies prohibit free gifts. It is important to avoid the perception that a CHOP workforce member could be influenced by free gifts from current or potential vendors. Our vendor policies are available on our Compliance intranet site. If you have additional questions, talk to your supervisor or the Office of Compliance and Privacy for guidance.

Work Area Scenarios

Let’s take a look at some scenarios when potential violations may occur.

Scenario 1: Patient Privacy

You have access to patient records as part of your job at CHOP. You heard a rumor that one of your neighbors was injured at your neighborhood playground and admitted to the Hospital. Even though you are not involved in your neighbor’s care, you are tempted to access the medical record in EPIC just to see if he is okay.


What should you do?

You should not access the patient record. At CHOP, we are committed to patient privacy, and no member of our workforce should ever access information about a patient or a patient’s family when it is for a purpose unrelated to performing your job at CHOP. Even if your intentions are good, it is still not appropriate to access a patient record for a non-work related purpose, including the location of a patient. You must contact the information desk for that information, just like any other visitor.

Scenario 2: Billing

You recently joined one of CHOP’s primary care network practices and are reviewing the records of patients that you will be taking over from Dr. Magneto. You notice in several of the records that Dr. Magneto has billed Medicaid for multiple MRIs and x-rays for patients with ailments that do not seem to require them. You do not see any documentation in the record to support these orders, nor can you find any images or radiology reports. You suspect that Dr. Magneto is billing for services that were never performed and you know that it is a physician’s responsibility to only bill for items and services in accordance with the law.


What should you do?

You should raise this concern with CHOP’s Chief Compliance Officer. You learned about the False Claims Act and are concerned that CHOP may suffer damages and reputational harm if Dr. Magneto bills for medically unnecessary procedures. As an employee, it is your responsibility to raise any compliance concerns, and improper billing.

When billing for services we:

  • Bill only for services and items actually provided
  • Bill only for medically necessary services
  • Use accurate coding when billing
  • Follow relevant rules when submitting requests for government payment in other contexts, such as research grants

Laws like the Federal False Claims Act prohibit fraudulent or otherwise improper billing or requests for payment. Any individual who knowingly or recklessly submits a false claim for payment to a federal or state healthcare program may be subjected to fines and penalties. See Compliance Standards of Conduct for more detail.
