The National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health (NIOSH) defines workplace violence as any physical assault, threatening behavior or verbal abuse occurring in the workplace. Violence includes overt and covert behaviors ranging in aggressiveness from verbal harassment to murder.

The Children's Hospital of Philadelphia has a zero tolerance policy for acts of violence or threats of violence in the workplace. All employees of the CHOP community are responsible for reporting any direct or perceived threats or other matters that the individual believes may result in harm to people or property in the workplace.

AWARENESS OF YOUR SURROUNDINGS

As a CHOP employee, it’s important to be aware simply due to the nature of the services we provide. In terms of workplace safety, awareness is also key. Be mindful of people who display Behaviors of Concern. These behaviors include the following:

  • Obsessions
  • Act of blaming others or holding grudges
  • Prolonged anger
  • Extreme and/or prolonged anxiety, depression, or sadness
  • Hypersensitivity to criticism
  • Angry outbursts
  • Intimidation and bullying
  • Signs of domestic violence
  • Sudden changes in behavior
  • Sudden creation of distance between an individual and others

Reporting Behaviors of Concern

If there is an imminent threat on Main Campus, contact security. All off campus locations and business occupancies should call 911.

Immediately report concerns to:

1Security – Ext. 4-5500

2Your Manager or Supervisor

3Human Resources

4CHOP's EAP (Employee Assistance Program)

MYTHS OF WORKPLACE VIOLENCE

Have you ever said or heard any of these phrases?

It happened out of the blue.

...He just snapped.

...If left alone, events will resolve themselves.

...We can't do anything to stop it.

...It couldn’t happen here.

Those phrases are myths of workplace violence. If you find yourself thinking or saying these things to yourself or others, work on your awareness.

Workplace violence always has a cause or reason. Don’t ignore it! Issues do not go away on their own and typically escalate. Learn how and when to recognize and diffuse potentially violent situations.
