The Importance of Patient Privacy

Our patients and families trust The Children’s Hospital of Philadelphia (CHOP) to care for them and to protect the information related to their care. Violating the confidentiality of our patients' information damages that trust and CHOP’s reputation.

State and federal laws (such as HIPAA – the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act), as well as CHOP policies and professional ethics, set clear boundaries regarding the use and sharing of patient information. Protecting our patients’ privacy is more than just a professional and legal obligation - it is the right thing to do.

Privacy has always been important in healthcare. In fact, it has been part of the Hippocratic Oath – the oath many physicians take when they enter medical practice – for centuries:

“Whatever I shall see or hear in the course of my profession, if it be what should not be disclosed, I will never divulge, holding such things to be holy secret.”

Patient Information That Is Confidential and Must Be Protected

Patient information that is confidential and must be protected includes information about CHOP patients, whether spoken, written, or electronic that:

  • May identify a patient or his or her relatives, employers, or household members
  • Is created or received by CHOP
  • Relates to a patient’s past, present, or future physical/mental health or condition, healthcare, or payment for healthcare

All of the following are examples of patient information that must be protected:

Demographics: patient name, address, e-mail address

Dates: birth date, admission date, discharge date, and date of death

Numbers: social security number, medical record number, health plan beneficiary number, billing account number, vehicle identifier/serial number, including license plate number, device identifier and serial number, certificate/license number, phone number, and fax number

Unique Characteristics: facial photographs, finger and voice prints, and/or any other unique identifying number, characteristic, or code

Other: Web Universal Resource Locators (URL) and Internet Protocol (IP) address

Did You Know?

Patient information exists in many different forms and places:

  • E-mail, electronic health record systems (e.g., EPIC), computer hard drives, network drives, USB storage devices, etc.
  • Paper documents such as prescriptions, discharge summaries, visit letters, patient schedules, patient ID bands, billing statements, etc.
  • Conversations with other care providers or patients and their families whether in person or over the phone

Privacy Rule

All patient information you come in contact with at CHOP is confidential. Take action to protect it from loss, theft, or unauthorized access or release.

Use & Sharing of Patient Information

Many people at CHOP must access, use, and share patient information every day to perform their jobs. Understanding what can be done with patient information is essential. You should NEVER access patient information unless it is necessary to perform your job.

A patient’s health information may be used or shared without written authorization for certain activities including:

  • Medical treatment
  • Payment for services
  • Healthcare operations (the day-to-day management of the hospital) such as quality assurance reviews and patient family complaint investigation and resolution
  • Compliance with rules and regulations that require sharing of patient information with:
    • Health departments to report infectious diseases or conditions
    • Child welfare agencies to report suspected child abuse or neglect
    • Attorneys, judges, and law enforcement officers responding to subpoenas and court orders

Privacy Rule

You may only access, use, or share patient information when necessary to do your job. If not related to your job, the activity is a violation of Hospital policy and can result in disciplinary action up to and including termination and/or removal from the medical and research staffs, as appropriate, depending on the seriousness of the violation.

Written patient authorization is REQUIRED for:

  • Release of patient information outside of CHOP such as sharing information directly with a patient’s school, daycare provider, or camp
  • Access, use, or sharing of patient information by CHOP staff for a purpose that is not for treatment, payment, or healthcare operations (or otherwise permitted by law)

Did You Know?

CHOP monitors access to electronic patient records for unusual activity to determine if the user’s access is not job-related. Individuals who choose to access patient information for any reason not related to their job risk disciplinary action, which can include termination under appropriate circumstances.

Some categories of patient information are considered especially sensitive and may have additional protections under federal and state laws, even beyond HIPAA. Some examples include:

  • Mental health treatment
  • HIV-related information
  • Drug or alcohol treatment
  • Testing and genetic information released to certain non-healthcare providers

If you come in contact with any of these types of especially sensitive information, consult your supervisor or the Privacy Office regarding additional requirements.

Minimum Necessary Rule

For purposes other than treatment, the minimum amount of patient information necessary to accomplish your task should be used. For example, sharing a patient safety story should not include information that identifies the patient.

Privacy Breach Prevention

A privacy breach can occur whenever patient information is seen or heard by someone who is not authorized to see or hear it. Privacy breaches can have serious consequences:

Legal and Enforcement Actions

Violations can result in government actions, including fines and sanctions.

Trust and Reputation

Under certain circumstances CHOP must inform patients who were affected by a privacy breach, the federal government, and sometimes, the media. This can negatively affect our patients’ and the public’s trust as well as CHOP’s reputation.

Audits

The federal government audits healthcare organizations to assure compliance with privacy rules.

Be Careful with Patient Information!

Since 2009, CHOP has notified 2,536 patients that their health information was compromised because of:

  • Lost paper records
  • Stolen, unencrypted USB drive
  • Wrongful access to patient records unrelated to a staff person’s job
  • Patient information posted on-line
  • Paper documents provided to the wrong patient family or sent in error by mail, fax, or email to a third party

You Can Help Prevent Privacy Incidents!

Remember YOU are the key to protecting our patients’ privacy.

Most privacy incidents can be avoided if you:

1

Always physically protect laptops, smartphones, and paper documents containing patient information from loss or theft.

2

Never store patient information on your home computer or any devices not managed by CHOP or on any third party “cloud” storage sites such as Drop Box, iCloud, or Google Drive.

3

Always keep your CHOP user ID and password private. Never share your password with anyone.

4

Always use two patient identifiers (e.g., patient name, medical record number, date of birth, etc.) when releasing documents with patient information to ensure the information is being shared with the correct person.

5

When emailing patient information, DOUBLE CHECK that you are sending it to the correct recipient.

6

When emailing patient information outside of CHOP, always type [SEND SECURE] (be sure to include brackets) in the subject line to encrypt the email and protect the contents. This is not necessary when sending emails within CHOP.

7

If you must remove or transport paper documents containing patient information between CHOP locations, use a CHOP courier or a locked transport case if transporting personally.

8

Dispose of paper documents containing patient information by shredding or placing them in a HIPAA bin.

9

Only look at patient records when needed to perform your job. It is wrong to look at a patient record for any other reason.

Reporting an Incident and Minimizing the Impact

Despite our best efforts, accidents will happen and privacy incidents will occur. When they do, it is your job to report the incident and take steps to minimize the impact.

You must report incidents that involve a potential breach of patient privacy to the Privacy Office. Some common privacy incidents to report include:

  • Paper documents provided to the wrong family (e.g., after visit summaries, prescriptions, medical records, visit letters, etc.)
  • Misdirected emails (regardless of whether the person who receives the email is a CHOP employee or someone outside the institution)
  • Faxes sent to the wrong person or fax number
  • Lost or stolen paper documents
  • Lost or stolen electronic devices, including your laptop or personal smartphone if managed by CHOP
  • Inappropriate access to electronic patient information (by you or someone else)

You should take prompt action to minimize the harm caused by a potential breach of patient privacy.

Review the examples below:

If you give a paper document to the wrong person or family:

  • Ask the recipient not to share the information and to return the document (send them a return envelope with postage paid if necessary), or confirm in writing that the document was destroyed.

If you send an email containing patient information to the wrong party:

  • Ask the recipient not to open or share the email.
  • Ask the recipient to confirm in writing that the email has been deleted.

If you fax a document with patient information to the wrong number:

  • Ask the recipient to destroy the document(s) without sharing them and provide confirmation.

If your CHOP-managed device, such as a laptop or smartphone, is lost or stolen:

  • Report the incident immediately to both the Privacy Office and Information Security (4-HELP). CHOP may be able to wipe the data or retrieve the device.

Patient Rights to Health Information

Did You Know?

CHOP’s Notice of Privacy Practices can be found in the following locations:

  • Any CHOP registration desk
  • The wall in patient waiting areas
  • CHOP’s public web site (search for HIPAA or Privacy)
  • CHOP’s intranet (search for HIPAA or Privacy)

CHOP’s Notice of Privacy Practices explains the rights patients have regarding their health information, and explains how they can exercise these rights. These rights include the right to:

  • Receive a copy of our Notice of Privacy Practices.
  • Review and obtain a copy of the patient’s record.
  • Request a change to information in the patient’s record.
  • Obtain a list of certain disclosures of information made by CHOP from the patient record.
  • Request a restriction on how CHOP may use and share the patient’s information.
  • Request special confidential communications from CHOP (i.e. receiving phone calls only at home and not at work).
  • Revoke a previously signed authorization to share the patient’s information.
  • Receive notice from CHOP of a breach of the patient’s information.
  • File a privacy complaint with CHOP or directly with the federal government, without being subject to intimidation or retaliation.
Adolescent Patient Rights

Adolescent patients under 18 years of age have a right to control how some of their healthcare information is shared, even with their own parents or guardians.

  • This includes healthcare information about HIV, sexually transmitted diseases, mental health issues, drug dependencies, and pregnancy.

Some adolescent patients (for example, those who are married or have been pregnant) have the same rights as adults, including control of their healthcare information.

Written authorization from the adolescent patient is required to share this information with a parent, guardian, or some third parties.

CAUTION!
Adolescent patients may control release of their health information. If you work with adolescents and have questions, ask your supervisor or contact the Privacy Office.
