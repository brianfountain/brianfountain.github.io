THE IMPORTANCE OF DATA PROTECTION

Patients and their families come to CHOP to receive world-class healthcare. Providing this level of care requires that we protect the confidential information of our patients, our workforce members, and our institution. Protecting both patient and institutional information is required by CHOP policy as well as state and federal laws. It is also the right thing to do.

Failure to protect confidential information can cause harm to our patients and our institution.

It is the responsibility of each of us to follow data protection behaviors to keep all CHOP confidential information private and secure.

The Data We Protect

Confidential Information includes information about CHOP's patients, members of the CHOP workforce, and the institution itself, whether it is oral, written, or electronic.

Patient Information

Patient information includes any information that may identify an individual and is created or received by the Hospital and relates to past, present, or future health or condition, healthcare, or payment for healthcare of the individual.

Some examples include:
  • Demographics: patient name, initials, address, e-mail address, phone, or fax number
  • Dates: birth, admission, discharge, and death
  • Numbers: social security numbers, medical record numbers, health plan beneficiary numbers, billing account numbers, vehicle or device numbers, certificate, and license numbers
  • Unique characteristics: facial photographs, finger or voice prints, or other unique characteristics, codes, or numbers that could identify a patient
  • Other: web URL or Internet Protocol (IP) address associated with the patient
Did You Know?

In some circumstances, adolescent patients control the release of their health information, even to their parents. Find out more about patient rights in CHOP's Notice of Privacy Practices.

Institutional Information

Institutional information includes any information that involves Hospital workforce members, (that is not deemed public information), non-public hospital business or strategy, non-public research, vendor trade secrets, or other confidential Hospital information.

Some examples include:
  • Confidential components of an employee's Human Resources record: social security numbers, driver's license numbers, and financial account information
  • Payroll and performance records: payroll records, salary information, and employee performance reviews
  • Strategy and future plans: Long range financial plans, Hospital strategy, and written legal advice
  • Research information: Research Inventions, Research trial protocol results, and CHOP intellectual property
Did You Know?

All CHOP clinical or business units that handle CHOP confidential information are required to create and maintain an inventory based on the Information Handling Standard. Documented inventories must include:

  • The type and volume of patient or institutional information
  • The classification of the information, business or clinical purpose
  • System(s) or location(s) that store the information
  • Individual accountable for the information

These inventories are critical for CHOP to know where its confidential data resides and can help all CHOP workforce members in securing it properly.

Laws, Regulations, and Organizations

All Hospital workforce members are required to abide by the laws, regulations, and accreditation standards that apply to CHOP's confidential information.

HIPAA/HITECH (The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act/The Health Information Technology for Economic and Clinical Health Act) are federal regulations that protect the privacy and security of patient information. The law requires organizations that fail to protect patient information to notify the affected patients, the government, and in some cases, the media. This law can also carry stiff penalties for organizations that fail to comply.

STATE LAWS are in addition to Federal laws. States like Pennsylvania and New Jersey have their own laws that require organizations who fail to protect the confidential information of patients, consumers and workforce members to notify those individuals.

THE JOINT COMMISSION is an organization that accredits all teaching hospitals in the United States and Canada. It has strict requirements for hospitals to keep confidential information safe.

PCI-DSS (Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard) is a standard for organizations that handle credit and debit card information. CHOP must adhere to PCI standards in order to process payment card transactions.

DATA BREACHES

Data Breaches in Healthcare

A data breach is an incident in which patient information is seen or heard by an individual not authorized to see or hear it. Data breaches occur whenever the confidentiality of patient information is compromised. In calendar year 2014 alone, over 215 large healthcare breaches occurred in the United States. Each involved the information of at least 500 people and affected over 10 million individuals. Since 2009, the federal government reports that more than 41 million people were affected by large healthcare data breaches. This number does not include the nearly 150,000 individuals affected by smaller breaches each year.

Almost half of the large data breach incidents involved a lost or stolen portable device such as a laptop, USB drive, or external hard drive. In the majority of these cases, human error and lack of basic security practices were the cause of the incident.

Data Breaches at CHOP

In 2014, CHOP notified over 450 patient families that their information was involved in a data breach incident. Since 2009 when the data breach notification rule went into effect, CHOP has notified over 2,500 patient families of a data breach that occurred either at CHOP or at one of our vendors. These incidents remind us of the ongoing need to practice data protection behaviors in our day-to-day handling of CHOP confidential information.

Did You Know?

Simply following good data security practices, as described in this training module, greatly reduces the likelihood of a data breach occurring.

Security Incidents

Information Security and CIA

When it comes to information security, keep CIA (Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability) in mind.

Confidentiality means protecting information from unauthorized access, use, or disclosure.

Integrity means protecting information from accidental or malicious modification or deletion.

Availability means ensuring timely and reliable access to information by authorized individuals.

Security Incidents

A security incident is unauthorized access, use, modification, disclosure, or destruction of CHOP confidential information, or interference with the operations of a CHOP information system. Security incidents result from:

1

Unintentional actions or human errors such as releasing confidential patient information to the wrong party.

2

Intentional actions such as accessing patient or institutional information unrelated to your job responsibilities.

3

Failing to practice basic data protection behaviors that protect the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of CHOP information.

Security incidents involving patient or workforce member information can easily lead to a data breach which may require notification to affected individuals, the media, and the Office for Civil Rights (OCR).

Did You Know?

Threats to information stored electronically have the potential to compromise huge amounts of confidential information. However, many of the data breaches at CHOP involving patient information occur as a result of mismanagement of paper documents, resulting in information being shared with the wrong person.

It is important to remember that both electronic and paper information must be protected.

The Consequences of a Data Breach

Failure to protect confidential information can have serious consequences.

Trust and Reputation:

When the confidentiality of patient information is compromised, CHOP must inform those affected, the federal government and, at times, the media. This can negatively affect CHOP's reputation as well as our patients' and the public's trust.

Legal and Enforcement Actions:

Violations can result in government fines, sanctions, and even criminal charges. Individuals have even been imprisoned for using confidential patient information for personal gain.

Audits:

The federal government has conducted 115 audits of healthcare organizations, focusing on the protection of patient information, with an additional 350 audits planned. The government is in the process of implementing an ongoing audit program.

BEST PRACTICES

5 Good Behaviors for Protecting Data

The following are five good behaviors for practicing Data Protection at CHOP.

1

I will not share my CHOP user ID or password or provide it in response to an email request, even one that claims to come from the IS Support Center.

2

I will not allow anyone to use my ID badge, nor will I prop open doors or otherwise provide physical access to secure areas at CHOP to unauthorized individuals.

3

I will not click on suspicious or unknown links in email that could download malicious software or viruses to the CHOP network.

4

I will not post CHOP confidential information on social media sites.

5

I will report any known or suspected violations of these behaviors to the CHOP IS Support Center and/or Privacy Office.

Passwords

The use of strong passwords can slow or often defeat the various attack methods used to compromise a computer's security.

To make them easier to remember, users often use the same or similar passwords for all their applications and given a choice, most users select very simple and easy-to-remember passwords such as their birthday, their mother's maiden name, or the name of a relative. Short and simple passwords are relatively easy for attackers to determine via the following methods:

  • Guessing

    The attacker attempts to log on using the user's account by repeatedly guessing likely words and phrases such as their children's names, pet's names, their city of birth, and local sports teams.

  • Dictionary Attack

    The attacker uses an automated program that includes a text file of words or a string of characters. The program repeatedly tries different password attempts to log into the system until it finds the user's password. These attacks are often very successful when individuals use passwords like ‘123456' or common combinations like ‘password123'.

Your CHOP password must be eight characters and contain upper and lower case letters and numbers. Ideally, create a unique CHOP password using a passphrase (e.g., "data protection is for everyone at CHOP" = dpis4e1@CHOP) and do not use your CHOP password for any other personal accounts. Your CHOP password should not be written down or shared with anyone for any reason.

Practical Handling Requirements for Confidential Information

CHOP has a set of practical requirements for the storage, sharing, transport, and disposal of confidential information. Review each section below to learn more.

Storage:

1

Always save electronic information to a CHOP network drive. These drives are secure and backed up to reduce the potential for data loss.

2

Use only CHOP-managed portable devices (e.g., CHOP-managed laptops, tablets, and smart phones enrolled in the Mobile Device Management program) to access CHOP confidential information. Never store confidential information on non-CHOP-managed devices such as your personal home computer or an unencrypted flash drive.

3

Do not use "Cloud Storage" solutions, such as Dropbox, iCloud, or Google Documents, to store confidential information.

4

Avoid the use of USB flash drives for storing confidential information because they are easy to lose. If a USB flash drive must be used, make sure it is encrypted by plugging it into a CHOP Windows PC and following the prompts.

5

To prevent the loss, theft, damage, or unauthorized access of paper records and documents containing confidential information:

  • Lock file cabinets and file rooms
  • Keep logs to track document location when records are being transported between CHOP locations.

Sharing:

1

Always double check when sharing confidential information. Use two patient identifiers (e.g., patient name and date of birth) to assure that the right information is released to the right person. Taking one extra minute to double check email addresses, fax numbers, mailing addresses, etc., can help avoid the most commonly reported breaches of confidential information. See Safeguards for Release of Patient Information Job Aid.

2

If your job requires you to share confidential information with authorized personnel, be sure to share the minimum necessary to complete the required task. For example, do not share information about the nature of a clinical encounter when the only information required is the next appointment date.

3

Always type "[send secure]" in the subject line of an email to encrypt it before sending confidential information outside of CHOP. A password protected file attachment to an unencrypted email is NOT secure.

4

Never access or share patient records or any other confidential information if it is not necessary to do your job. CHOP monitors access to systems and inappropriate access has resulted in disciplinary action.

Transport:

1

When transporting confidential paper documents, use a CHOP courier service. If you choose to transport yourself, use a locked transport case and take steps to protect it from loss or theft (e.g., never leave the locked bag unattended in a parked car or a publicly accessible area).

2

Secure any CHOP-managed portable devices to prevent loss or theft. These devices should never be left unattended, shared or loaned to unauthorized individuals, including family members. When flying with your CHOP-managed device, stow it in your carry-on luggage and keep it on your person at all times.

Disposal:

1

Shred paper documents containing confidential information or dispose of them in a locked HIPAA bin.

2

Be cautious when disposing of non-paper items containing patient information (e.g., ID bracelets, IV bags, medication bottles). For detailed guidance, see the Disposal of Non-paper Items Containing Patient Identifiers Job Aid.

3

Simply deleting files from your computer or a storage device will not permanently erase those files. Contact the IS Support Center for assistance with permanently deleting files.

4

Use the correct procedures to dispose equipment and devices that may contain confidential information (e.g., old computers, copy machines, etc.). Call the IS Support Center to schedule the pickup and secure disposal of the devices.
